‘We spent a month working out of Himachal. Here’s how it went’

The internet was fast, the views were great and though they had to make a few adjustments, it all worked out wonderfully well for this Mumbai couple

By Garima Raghuvanshy

10 January 2021

I’m a small-town girl living in one of India’s biggest cities. I married a man with a similar story. And though we love our life in Mumbai, we spend part of it planning escapes to places with open spaces, big trees, and clear skies. Cooped up at home during the first phase of the lockdown, we thanked god for Mumbai’s fantastic food delivery services. But when India started opening up, we knew we wanted to make something more of the unexpected freedom that had been handed to us. Working from the mountains was a blissful prospect. Giddy with excitement, we got to planning.

Priority #1: High-speed internet

We wanted our escape to be truly relaxing, so we knew that nothing less than a month would do. We also wanted it to be a restorative experience, so we looked for places far, far removed from the madding crowds. Since this was to be a workation, high-speed broadband was our most important requirement. We asked every property we contacted to run speed tests on fast.com and send us screenshots of the results (Tip: Internet speed alone doesn’t guarantee seamless video calls. Make sure to ask for a screenshot of the latency data as well. Loaded latency of less than 100ms should be more than enough).

#2: The place

A close second on our list of requirements was an excellent view. We wanted to be sure that every time we looked up from our screens, we’d be blown away. This was a workation after all. A meals-inclusive package was our last important requirement. Neither my husband nor I have the discipline necessary for cooking on weekdays—we spent most of the lockdown eating ghee-rice. So expecting ourselves to work, enjoy the mountains, and cook, was expecting too much.

We also really wanted to enjoy a sense of space that is unaffordable in Mumbai. The thought of going from our cute but compact Mumbai flat to an even smaller hotel room did not excite, great views notwithstanding. We sure as hell can’t afford spaciousness in Mumbai, but we were going to try damned hard to find it at a reasonable price in the mountains.

And hence: Naggar, Himachal Pradesh

Budget, must-haves, and preferences considered, more than half the properties we liked turned out to be in a Himachali village called Naggar. This was intriguing, and the more I read about Naggar, the more I knew that this was where we’d be heading.

Located in Kullu valley, some 20km before Manali, is the old, picturesque village of Naggar. Once the capital of the Kullu kings, pace of life in Naggar carries little burden of this erstwhile eminence. Perched high up on a mountain side, Naggar has a commanding view of the valley below and the snow-capped mountains beyond. Everything in the village is within walking distance. It is small enough to have no Swiggy or Zomato, but large enough to have genuinely good coffee and a fairly authentic Italian restaurant. Every other day, drumbeats announce a festival, a visiting deity, or another wedding in the village. For my husband and I, both mountain lovers, one seeker of solitude, the other an avid student of culture, Naggar was perfect.

We got there on 3 November. Even at the onset of winter, Naggar really came to life only by 10am, after the sun had coaxed the grass from frozen crunchy to dewy fresh. By six in the evening, the night’s darkness settled in. By 8pm, almost everyone was indoors, gathered around the tandoor or snuggled next to a heater.

Naggar is awash with options for accommodation, from hotel and hostel rooms on the arterial road to artsy homestays perched high above the village. We chose the tastefully appointed studio loft at Himalayan Brothers Adventure Homestay (HBA). Even while being in the centre of the village, the homestay is tucked away into a corner of its own, surrounded by Apple trees and with a wonderful view of the mountains. Brothers Bhrigu and Ranbir Acharya run the warm, informal setup. We had the run of the place and everyone in the Acharya family soon became friends rather than just hosts. The homestay shares space with the Acharyas’ ancestral home, a kath kuni structure anywhere from 300-500 years old. Bhrigu is spearheading an initiative to restore this amazing house and transform it into a multidimensional heritage centre and experiential homestay (check out the The Naggar Project).

Realities of a workation

I write—not entirely for a living. But my work schedule is flexible. My husband’s work is much more structured. He has a calendar full of client calls and weekly connects with colleagues. But once the day is done, and on the weekends, he actively keeps his work out of his mind and our life. As you can imagine, his workdays and mine look very different. To be productive, though, we both need the same thing; a quiet space, where one person’s calls or breaks won’t disturb the other. Tip: While looking for a workation property, make sure you’ll have that—one or multiple quiet spaces you can use to make calls, read, and focus. In the same vein, make sure you’ll have access to individual workstations. A music producer, chartered accountant, content writer, and consultant working from the same picnic table are almost certainly going to get in each other’s way. And trust me, everyone who can is taking a workation. So make sure you’ll have a plug point, a table, and a chair to yourself.

While HBA in Naggar doesn’t have designated work areas, our verandah had a table and two chairs. This and the bar table inside the studio were my husband’s workstations. I would work from basically any spot in the sun. I often took calls in the field next to the homestay, and climbed into the hammock at the sunny end of our verandah when I wanted to read or write on my laptop. There were enough spare tables for me to set up a workstation when I needed one, but Naggar was already pretty cold in November and I vastly preferred spending sunlight hours baking my bones. Whenever I wanted a change of scene, or just multiple cups of coffee, I would take my work over to the charming Wool Café. They serve great brews and possibly the best cheesecake you will ever have. For my husband and I, evening coffee at the café quickly became a weekday ritual.

When working from a Himalayan village did present practical challenges, these were few and far between. Some nights got too cold for comfort. Burying myself blankets at 8pm was an effective solution, and a great way to fall asleep way too soon, but being too cold to do anything except huddle in bed got a little bit frustrating sometimes. For most part though, we were well prepared for the weather (thank you, clearly labelled negative temperature gear from Decathlon). Electricity was the other occasional concern. Even though Naggar doesn’t usually have power-outages, it is, after all, in the mountains. In the event of a snowstorm or a cloudburst, internet and electricity could be cut off, and possibly for more than a few hours. But because we were going to be in Naggar in November, just before the heavy snows of winter came in, we weren’t too worried about that contingency. The electricity did go out, but only thrice during our month-long stay, and each time, for not more than two hours. Luckily our laptops were charged and we could make do with mobile hotspots. Tip: if you plan to work from the mountains or from any place that isn’t a major city, carry a power bank or two, and keep your laptop well-charged most of the time for good measure.

Ideally, our workation in the mountains would have lasted two months instead of one. But personal commitments and the fact that we were still paying astronomical Mumbai rent meant we had to head back before we were 100% ready. Even so, a-month long stay in the mountains was an inconceivable luxury just six months ago. Now we know from experience that it’s doable. Until this year, our annual 10-day vacation had to accommodate family time, self-care, and travel. We’d often return from our vacations more tired than while heading out. Now that we have finally experienced the revelation that are long-stays, workationing might become an annual ritual, and the predominant way we explore India.

In many ways, our workation was exactly what we had wanted. But I also struggled a peculiar struggle. The tough part of my time in Naggar was finding the balance between work and vacation. I wanted to produce great work, but also to experience all the beauty this slice of the mountains had to offer. I wanted to hone my article drafts until they were sharp and incisive, but I also wanted to hike to ancient villages, eat the Pahari dishes of the region, and witness celebrations of at least a few local festivals. In an attempt to do both, I actually spent the first ten days or so anxious and exhausted. But sometime during our four weeks there, Naggar taught me that this really didn’t have to be a tightrope test. Watching the timeless mountains transform, sometimes several times in a single day, slowly a realisation began to strengthen. I was not going to do and experience everything. But whatever was happening, whatever I was doing, was pretty darned amazing.

Can a workation work out for you?

Working from Naggar did not change how we experienced work. We liked it as much or as little as before. But it did change our experience of working. We were surrounded by beauty. Our city skyline, crowded with skyscrapers and smoke, was swapped for crisp, expansive skies framed by cloud-kissed mountains. Busy traffic and blaring horns made way for leisurely walks and chirping birds. Most of our weekends were spent lying in the sun doing nothing. A few were spent hiking to stunning villages, trying out local delicacies, exploring the upper reaches of the Himalayas (courtesy the fabulous Atal Tunnel), and realising that real apple trees look nothing like the thick-trunked variety we used to draw in school. Removed from the factors that make life in a city exhausting, and dipped in the wonders of the Himalayas instead, our days in Naggar were dotted with moments of quiet joy, exuberant joy, and startlingly good lung health.

My husband likes to say that if you’re in the mountains long enough, they’ll make you happy one day or another. When he says that he’s talking about witnessing great sunsets, or snowfall, or full moon night. But I think he might be onto something more. I spent our last day in Naggar wandering around the village. On a whim, I walked over to the Nicholas Roerich Memorial House and Art Gallery. Having never heard of Roerich before researching Naggar, I hadn’t been particularly motivated to visit the gallery earlier in our stay. The fact that it was firmly on the tourist map didn’t help either. But on that last day, I walked over anyway. And I realised why the people of Naggar call him ‘Roerich ji’, a sant, a spiritual master. His shimmering, luminous Himalayan scenes filled me with bright, lingering joy. It was an experience I hadn’t associated with fine art before. The next morning, we drove off to the plains. I looked often into the rear-view mirror, departing glances at the gleaming peaks and emerald trees. But there was no regret, no twinge of sadness. My month in the mountains had made me happy.
